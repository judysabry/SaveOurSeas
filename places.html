Place markers · Sḵwx̱wú7mesh shore

The names that were here

Five markers along the Burrard Inlet shoreline that carry their original names. A welcome figure carved from cedar. A transformer rock. Two villages, one cleared, one being rebuilt. The Squamish, Tsleil-Waututh and Musqueam history of this coast does not begin in 1792, and most of it is still here if you know where to look.

Marker 01 · Ambleside Park Welcome Figure
Marker No. 01 · Carved Cedar

The Welcome FigureCoast Salish · Ambleside Park

Visitors walking along the waterfront at Ambleside Park often stop to admire the tall wooden Welcome Figure that stands proudly near the beach. The carving has become one of the most recognized landmarks in West Vancouver and reminds people of the deep Indigenous history connected to the land.

Long before roads, houses, and buildings were built, the shoreline around Ambleside was home to the Squamish people. The forests were thick with cedar trees, and the ocean was full of salmon, seals, and canoes traveling across the water. The Welcome Figure represents hospitality, respect, and connection to the community. Many people believe figures like this were traditionally placed near villages to greet visitors arriving by canoe.

The figure at Ambleside was carved from cedar wood, a tree that has been important to many Indigenous peoples on the West Coast for thousands of years. Cedar was used to build homes, clothing, tools, and canoes. Artists carefully carved designs into the wood to tell stories and represent family history, culture, and traditions.

Today, the Welcome Figure continues to teach visitors about Coast Salish culture and the importance of protecting Indigenous traditions.

By Trystan Paulin·Place marker 01·Ambleside Park
Marker 02 · South foot, Burrard Bridge Sen̓áḵw
Marker No. 02 · Rebuilt Village

Sen̓áḵwSquamish Nation · False Creek

Sen̓áḵw is a large new housing and community project being built by the Squamish Nation near the Burrard Bridge in Vancouver. The project will include thousands of homes, stores, parks, and public spaces. Many people think it could help with Vancouver's housing crisis by giving more people places to live.

Before European settlers came to Canada, the area was an important Squamish village along the shoreline. Indigenous families lived there for generations and used the land and water for fishing, canoe travel, and trade. The shoreline looked very different back then, with forests, beaches, and salmon-filled waters instead of roads and tall buildings.

In the early 1900s, many Squamish residents were forced to leave the area. Today, the Sen̓áḵw project is seen as a way for the Squamish Nation to reclaim and rebuild part of its historic community.

The project is also designed to support public transit, biking, and walking instead of heavy car use. Some people are excited about the new housing and businesses, while others worry about traffic and overcrowding. Even with different opinions, Sen̓áḵw is becoming an important symbol of Indigenous leadership and modern city planning in Canada.

Marker 03 · Stanley Park seawall Slhx̱í7lsh
Marker No. 03 · Transformer Story

Slhx̱í7lshSquamish oral history · Stanley Park

Slhx̱í7lsh is an important figure in Squamish oral history and storytelling. In many Indigenous stories from the Squamish people, Slhx̱í7lsh is described as a powerful transformer being who helped shape the land, animals, and rivers into how they are today. These stories are passed down through generations to teach lessons and explain the natural world.

In traditional teachings, Slhx̱í7lsh is not just a character in stories, but also part of a larger system of knowledge and culture. The stories often show how the world changed long ago, when animals and landforms could transform. Through these changes, lessons are shared about respect, balance, and the connection between people and nature.

Today, stories of Slhx̱í7lsh are still shared as part of Squamish cultural education and language learning. Elders and knowledge keepers use these stories to teach younger generations about identity, history, and respect for the land. They are an important part of keeping the Squamish language and culture alive.

Even though these stories are very old, they are still meaningful today. They help connect people to the past and remind communities of the deep relationship between land, water, and life in the Coast Salish world.

Marker 04 · Stanley Park (Lumberman's Arch) Xwáyxway
Marker No. 04 · Cleared Village

XwáyxwaySḵwx̱wú7mesh village · Stanley Park

Xwáyxway was an important village and cultural site of the Squamish Nation people, also known as the Sḵwx̱wú7mesh Nation. It was located in what is now Stanley Park in Vancouver. Long before the city of Vancouver existed, Xwáyxway was a thriving community where Indigenous families lived, gathered food, held ceremonies, and shared stories.

The village was surrounded by forests, beaches, and rich waters filled with salmon, shellfish, and other resources. Cedar trees were especially important because they were used to build homes, canoes, clothing, and tools. The people of Xwáyxway had a deep connection to the land and water and cared for the environment for thousands of years.

In the late 1800s, European settlement greatly changed the area. When Stanley Park was created in 1888, many Indigenous people living in Xwáyxway were forced to leave their homes. Even though the village itself no longer exists, its history and cultural importance are still remembered by the Squamish people and other Coast Salish Nations.

Today, Xwáyxway remains an important symbol of Indigenous history and resilience. Efforts continue to teach people about the original inhabitants of the land and the lasting culture of the Sḵwx̱wú7mesh people. Learning about places like Xwáyxway helps people better understand the true history of Vancouver and the importance of respecting Indigenous communities and traditions.

Marker 05 · Capilano River, North Vancouver X̱wemelch'stn
Marker No. 05 · Living Community

X̱wemelch'stn"Fast Moving Water of Fish" · Capilano

X̱wemelch'stn is one of the oldest and most important communities of the Squamish Nation, also known as the Sḵwx̱wú7mesh Úxwumixw. The community is located near the Capilano River in North Vancouver and is sometimes called the Capilano Reserve. The name X̱wemelch'stn means "Fast Moving Water of Fish," which refers to the nearby river and the abundance of salmon that once filled its waters.

Long before Vancouver was founded, X̱wemelch'stn was a large and thriving Coast Salish village. Families lived in longhouses made from cedar wood and relied on the land and water for food, transportation, and trade. Salmon, shellfish, cedar trees, and the forests around Burrard Inlet were all important parts of daily life. The village was also an important gathering place for Squamish people and leaders.

When European settlers arrived in the 1800s, life changed greatly for the people of X̱wemelch'stn. Reserve lands were created, and many Indigenous communities lost access to parts of their traditional territory. Despite these challenges, the Squamish people continued to protect their language, culture, and traditions.

Today, X̱wemelch'stn remains one of the largest and most active Squamish Nation communities. It is home to families, schools, cultural programs, and community centres that help keep Squamish traditions strong for future generations. Learning about X̱wemelch'stn helps people understand the deep Indigenous history of the North Shore and the continuing presence of the Squamish Nation in the region.

A note on these names

Spelling, pronunciation, and respect

The diacritical marks in Sḵwx̱wú7mesh names matter. They aren't decoration. The 7, the underline, the ḵ, the apostrophe — each one is a sound the Latin alphabet doesn't carry on its own. Learning to write them correctly is the first small thing a settler can do to acknowledge the language was here first.

Pronunciation guides are published by the Sḵwx̱wú7mesh Nation and the Tsleil-Waututh Nation. The Squamish-Lil'wat Cultural Centre in Whistler runs language workshops open to the public. If you live on this coast, learning to say Sen̓áḵw and X̱wemelch'stn correctly is a generous use of an afternoon.

The descriptions on this page are introductory, written for visitors. The fuller stories belong to the Nations themselves and are taught by their knowledge keepers. Where you can, listen to the people whose stories these are.

These pages are written on the unceded, ancestral and traditional lands of the Sḵwx̱wú7mesh Úxwumixw (Squamish Nation), səlilwətaɬ (Tsleil-Waututh Nation), and xʷməθkʷəyə̓ m (Musqueam Nation).
