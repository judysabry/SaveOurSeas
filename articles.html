Dispatch No. 01 · The Long Look

Counting what is gone

The Capilano chum used to run so thick you could walk across the inlet on their backs. A century of dams, log booms, and overfishing has reduced their numbers to a remnant. The volunteers counting the few that return are not optimistic. They are just stubborn.

By E. Hartley · 2,400 words · 11 min read · Filed October 2025 · revised May 2026

The men who counted salmon at the Capilano in the 1880s didn't bother with math. They wrote down descriptions like abundant, or past counting. When the year was particularly good, the entire pool would turn a deep color and stay that way until the rains came in November. We have lost both the language and the fish. Now, modern returns are reported as exact figures with an error margin. This precision feels like an apology for what's left.

In the autumn of 2025, the West Vancouver Streamkeepers counted 279 returning coho along the local creeks and 72 chinook. It was the second-highest count since the volunteer program began. A small victory. For anyone keeping track over the years, this number would have fit into a single morning's catch at the Capilano mouth in 1912.

A century of subtraction

The first dam was built on the Capilano in 1888 to support the growing North Shore. A second, larger dam followed in 1954. From the salmon's perspective, the river above the second dam no longer functions as a river. Each fall, fish that reach the lower section are trucked over the dam in tanks by the hatchery staff. Their offspring are raised in pens before being released downstream. It works, but biologically, it's a fragile situation.

The notes on the 1880s row are honest. Nobody really knows the true numbers. Some cannery records survive, but they only detail the fish that were caught and shipped, not the entire run. The numbers we use now are based on canning rates and gear lists, which is like measuring a river by counting the buckets people took from it.

What matters is the pattern. By any reasonable measure, the inlet has lost about ninety-five percent of its salmon biomass since the canneries opened. The grizzly bears that fished the lower reaches of every North Shore creek in the 1880s are now gone. The bald eagles returned in the 1990s and are thriving. The salmon, for the most part, are not.

What if

The most thorough attempt to envision a recovery I found is a 2019 paper from UBC. It modeled what Burrard Inlet salmon populations could look like without pressure from commercial fishing, with full passage at the dams, intact estuaries, and a restored lower Fraser plume. The numbers it produced were not equal to those from 1880. The authors concluded that historic abundance is almost certainly gone. However, the projected returns were ten to thirty times the current counts. Past numbers are lost, but heavy returns might be within reach.

"I don't think people quite understand how many fish were here, even within living memory. My grandfather told me about catching coho on a hand line off the Ambleside pier in the fifties. You couldn't keep them out of your bucket. Try that today." Stan Joseph, Squamish Nation, in conversation, 2024

The $49 million rebuild of the Capilano Hatchery, which will begin this autumn, will not achieve that alone. It will maintain the current program, which is crucial. The rest depends on wild stocks recovering, side channels being restored, and habitats in the lower reaches being improved. This is volunteer work, ultimately carried out by streamkeepers. These are the people who walk the creeks and count.

Sources Fisheries and Oceans Canada · BC Salmon Restoration Fund · West Vancouver Streamkeepers Society · North Shore News, "Surveys find resilient salmon populations" (2025) · Connors et al., Canadian Journal of Fisheries and Aquatic Sciences, 2019.
Dispatch No. 02 · The Underwater Meadow

cə́ləm

For the longest time eelgrass was the invisible connective tissue of the Burrard Inlet: it provided shelter for salmon and herring fry, held the seafloor in place, and acted as an unseen sink for carbon. The Tsleil-Waututh Nation are working to re-establish it, one transplant at a time, four thousand shoots per year.

By R. Pemberton · 1,900 words · 9 min read · Filed April 2026

cə́ləm is the name for eelgrass in the Squamish language; Zostera marina is its Latin appellation, correct, but unhelpful, as it's not a grass. It's one of the few flowering plants that has ever re-colonised the oceans. The blades are long and ribbon-like and form meadows in the shallow waters of the inlets, knee-deep when the tide is out. If you've ever waded the flats below the bridge and felt something soft under your feet, that was likely it.

Years ago, one could have walked from Ambleside Park east along the shore for a couple of hours without being out of sight of one of its meadows; today, nearly none of it remains. Most of what does is what the Tsleil-Waututh Nation has put back.

Why Eelgrass

The point of the eelgrass is what is hidden beneath it. It serves as a nursery for juvenile salmon and herring, provides cover for crabs, and is used for nesting. Indeed, all the shallow-water fish, including Dungeness and rock crab, sand lance and surf smelt, which make up the bulk of both their own and everyone else's fishing, rely, to some degree, on eelgrass beds.

Eelgrass also performs the more arduous, though unnoticed, task of stabilising the seabed at high tide and slowing down wave action as it approaches the shore. By absorbing nitrogen and phosphorus from the water column (which in olden times used to come from the forest and today from suburbia lawns) and, increasingly, by locking up carbon (the current catch-phrase funding agencies need to hear), a square metre of mature eelgrass meadow holds as much carbon as the same area of temperate forest in a place in the world that the forests cannot reach.

The Transplants

The process appears low-tech. A person wades into the water and takes one shoot from the existing bed, carefully not to damage the neighbouring roots. A small, biodegradable mat (often burlap and a stone) is attached to the base, and a diver carries it down and plants the shoot into the silt. Then you wait. You come back in a year and do a count.

In 2024 the Tsleil-Waututh Nation, in partnership with the marine conservation group SeaChange, completed a four-year project in Burrard Inlet. Over four thousand shoots were planted in the flats near their reserve, as well as at Whey-ah-Wichen/Cates Park. The largest single transplant was 125,000 shoots, placed near the Port of Vancouver in Maplewood; the largest one yet in Burrard Inlet.

The transplants survive; in general, first-year survival used to be abysmal, over 50 per cent, but the results from Cates Park most recently show 70 percent first-year survival, and established beds appear to be self-spreading. Progress is proceeding much more rapidly than funders had imagined.

"We're not trying to bring back what was, rather enabling the inlet to bring back what it can. The eelgrass knows what to do." TWN Stewardship Department briefing, Spring 2024

The visible shoreline hasn't dramatically altered in Ambleside, but underwater it has been changed dramatically. Swimming out from the pier on a bright July day, at about 10 feet of water, in a bright patch, long green ribbons have appeared where only sand was seen just five years ago.

Dispatch No. 03 · Sen̓áḵw

Back home

The Province of British Columbia, working with the federal government, loaded up people from Sen̓áḵw and expelled them from their village at gunpoint in 1913. The fire that followed erased nearly everything. The Squamish Nation is now rebuilding the village in its original location more than 100 years later.

By M. Howe · 2,600 words · 12 minute read · Published March 2026

There's an odd kind of shame when walking along the south end of the Burrard Bridge today. There's almost nothing that reveals the former village: there is the seawall, a dog park, and the Granville Island parking lot. Nothing here announces the fact that a settlement existed here for millennia, a village, complete with longhouses, smokehouses, ceremonial spaces, and gardens, before being set ablaze and cleared by government order in May 1913.

The Squamish name for the site is Sen̓áḵw. It was known as Snauq during settler times, before the Province assigned it numbers: Reserve Six, later Indian Reserve Six. After the residents had been forced out, it ceased to be a designation at all, and for 90 years, the site was merely land under an off-ramp.

What was there

In the mid-1850s, Sen̓áḵw was among the largest Squamish settlements on the south coast. In winter, the area served as the main gathering site for several families whose seasonal fishing camps dotted Howe Sound and the adjacent inlet. Sen̓áḵw benefited from the abundant salmon, herring, clams, and seabirds that drew people to the lower mainland. Significantly, at a time when reservation policy favored removal and assimilation, Sen̓áḵw also possessed a thriving economy. The village traded smoked salmon both with the inland communities and with the new, all-white settlement established on the opposite side of the inlet after 1860.

The numbers appear stark. Between 1850 and 1900, the population dropped from 400 to zero. Within the space of less than ten years, it leaped from zero to 9,000. The newly rebuilt Sen̓áḵw is not intended as a recreation, but as an urban development project (developed by Westbank in conjunction with the Nation) consisting of eleven residential high-rises, to be constructed on the original land. It is an urban endeavor rather than a historical site.

What if we'd stayed

It is instructive, albeit difficult, to contemplate the alternative: what if 1913 hadn't happened? What if the village had been allowed to remain? What if the federal cut-off laws had not been applied here? The smokehouses would still be working; the shell-midden beneath the picnic tables would be undisturbed.

Imagining such a scenario without romanticizing it is an exercise. The economic pressures of settling a million acres of beachfront urban land were real, and would not have simply disappeared. However, communities where removals were not absolute or where resistance lasted longer, like Musqueam's holdings at the mouth of the Fraser River or the Tsleil-Waututh reserve at the head of the inlet, offer a potential image of a Sen̓áḵw in 2026: a functioning winter village of hundreds of inhabitants, a marine-based economy, and a much less concrete coastline.

"Our people did not agree to leave. The land was taken. We are not asking to come back. We are building." Khelsilem, Squamish Nation Council Chair, 2022

The new Sen̓áḵw is now the largest First Nations development project in Canadian history and is predicted to be, upon completion, 20 times more populous than the village was in 1850. This is not what was lost. It is, at this point in the land's narrative, both true and inaccurate.

Dispatch No. 04 · The Eight Hundred Year Tree

Eight hundred years

An old western red cedar lives eight centuries. The North Shore used to have a solid forest of them, from ridge to tide. Only 2.5% of this forest is still standing.

By I. Calloway · 1,800 words · 8 min read · Filed February 2026

At the southeastern end of Lighthouse Park, about a fifteen-minute hike in from the parking lot, you can reach out and touch a cedar. It is over three meters in diameter at chest height, and the actual age is difficult to know (an increment borer won't go all the way through a tree this large), but the park's biologist tentatively estimates its age between 600 and 700 years. It was likely already a sapling when Sen̓áḵw's smokehouses were operational.

So far as I know, it's the oldest living thing you can reach within a thirty-minute walk of downtown Vancouver. There are perhaps a hundred similar trees remaining on the North Shore. Once, there were uncounted.

A functioning cedar

At minimum, an old cedar holds up a lot of stuff. Rain is channeled down deep vertical furrows in the bark and nourishes the layer of moss. The lower branches wither away over decades, becoming hollows that serve as nests for owls, fishers, marbled murrelets, sapsuckers, and bats. The fibrous mat of the root system spreads out fifteen or twenty meters past the visible trunk and anchors the entire slope it is situated on. When a cedar of this size eventually falls, it continues to do the work of its existence for another two or three hundred years as nurse log. A coastal forest is not an assemblage of trees, but rather a single, connected ecosystem. Live and dead elements fulfill the same functions.

The remaining old-growth forest on the North Shore is distributed among three pockets: Lighthouse Park (75 ha), the watersheds of the upper Lynn and Capilano Rivers (sealed by Metro Vancouver's gates), and a handful of unsurveyed gullies in the alpine regions that were not worth logging. All other areas consist of second-growth stands. While beautiful, none are currently functioning in the way an 800-year-old forest does.

If we'd left them

Had there still been a continuous forest of cedars running from the ridge top to the tide line, it would have helped prevent mudslides (since the root systems hold the slopes), kept the shoreline streams clear of silt, fostered an understory dense enough to control the deer population and support the populations of small mammals that are now functionally absent, and the trees themselves would be growing: trees 12 meters in diameter at breast height would have been common, and many would be old enough to have seen the eruption of Mount Mazama that left the ash layer detectable today.

"People walk to Lighthouse Park, stop in front of that big tree and get quiet. That's the response you're looking for. Used to be you could find trees like that from here right out to the inlet." Park biologist, Lighthouse Park, during a conversation in 2024

None of this can be reclaimed within our lifetimes. A tree planted today will be only a small one when it's a century old, assuming optimal care. However, the question of whether or not to plant, to commit to the North Shore as a region to a thousand-year time frame, remains open. The District of North Vancouver's Urban Forest Strategy has established targets for tree canopy coverage of 40% by 2050. The Squamish Nation has included long-rotation cedar regeneration in its lease agreements on the Capilano watershed. Individually, neither of these commitments achieves a full remedy. Taken together, over centuries, these could.

Dispatch No. 05 · Rest

Rest

A beach is a working ecosystem. We tend to use it as a leisure good twelve months a year. The case for letting the shoreline have a season off is not romantic. It is simply how it used to recover.

By D. Sandys · 1,500 words · 7 min read · Filed January 2026

The first dog of the day at Ambleside arrives at about six-fifteen in the morning. A retriever, usually. Her person carries a coffee in a paper cup and lets her run the wet sand. By seven there are six dogs, sometimes ten. The off-leash zone runs from the foot of the pier east to the edge of the park. The dogs use roughly seven hundred metres of beach a day. So do the gulls, the crows, the river otters, and so, increasingly less often, do the surf scoters and the sand-bottom invertebrates that the scoters used to come for.

There is, in coastal ecology, a quiet body of research on what is called disturbance pressure. Most of it is North Atlantic, conducted on European holiday beaches that close in the off-season. The findings are unambiguous. A beach with a winter rest period, three to four months when people and dogs and boats stay off it, recovers the small invertebrate fauna (worms, amphipods, copepods) that the next year's seabirds and juvenile fish depend on. A beach with no rest period does not.

The pre-contact pattern

The Squamish, the Tsleil-Waututh and the Musqueam did not leave the inlet unused in winter. They lived here, in winter villages. They harvested. But the pattern of harvest was seasonal and segmented. Different beaches were worked at different times. The shoreline below Sen̓áḵw, for example, was an active summer fishing and clam-bed site and a winter ceremonial one, almost never both at once. The fallow time was built into the year. Beaches got months at a time when the only footprint on them was the heron's.

That last row is the encouraging one. Maplewood is the only site on the inlet where bird counts have risen against the 1990 baseline. It is also the only one where the shoreline is closed to dogs and casual access, it is a managed conservation area, not a park.

A proposal that won't happen

The honest case for a December-to-March rest period at Ambleside, leashed dogs only, no off-leash zone, a roped-off section of the back beach, is straightforward, modest, and not going to be implemented. The off-leash beach is the most popular dog amenity in West Vancouver. It is one of the things that makes the neighbourhood the kind of neighbourhood it is. Asking residents to accept three months of leashed walking is, as a local councillor put it to me, "a perfectly good policy that will end three political careers".

"We are not asking for a closure. We are asking for a season. Just one season. The shoreline has been giving year-round for a hundred years and it cannot do that indefinitely." from a 2023 Coho Festival panel on shoreline ecology

A version of it might happen at Cates Park, where the Tsleil-Waututh have meaningful authority and the visitor numbers are smaller. It is, anyway, where the data will come from. If winter rest does measurable good there over three or four years, the political conversation around Ambleside will be different. The science is settled. The politics is the slow part.

Dispatch No. 06 · Soft Edges

When the concrete comes down

Roughly two-thirds of Burrard Inlet's shoreline, seawall, riprap, concrete fill. Marine biologists call this the coastal squeeze. Very slowly, a small movement has started to unbuild sections of it.

By E. Hartley · 2,200 words · 10 min read · Filed May 2026

A natural coastal shoreline is a gentle stack of zones in cross-section. The intertidal flat, rising to pebble and gravel beach, then storm wrack and drift wood, then salt tolerant dune plants, then forest. Each zone contains a different community of life. The combination of zones make up a coast. A seawall is one vertical surface, replacing five natural zones.

Almost two-thirds of the shoreline of Burrard Inlet, from the Burrard bridge across the whole southern edge, much of the northern shore beyond Ambleside, the whole of the working harbour; it's all now seawall, riprap or concrete fill. The original five zones have been compressed or eliminated by the wall. The biologists call this coastal squeeze. Surveys have estimated an almost 90% decrease in invertebrate species, and a near-total absence of juvenile fish habitat where it's been measured.

Soft armouring

Most coastlines do need protection from the winter storms. "No protection," is the not opposite of seawall. The opposite of a seawall is what engineers call "soft armouring", a engineered beach that functions similarly in wave attenuation to a seawall but is a gently sloping artificial beach made of gravel and rocks topped with dune grass.

The Ambleside Pier sub-tidal habitat project completed in 2015 represents the District of West Vancouver's closest foray into a public soft armouring project. Large rocks were added to the area just below the pier at low tide level to achieve two things, mitigate the wave energy impacting the beach and give salmon, herring, surf smelt, rockfish and Dungeness crab a refuge they had lost when the original boulder field was cleared for the ferry landing a century ago.

Ten years later, the reefs are working. The District's monitoring counts show measurable returns of all the target species, including a small but real Dungeness population that was effectively absent at the site in the decade before installation. The reefs cost roughly $480,000. Replacing the entire 2.4-kilometre Ambleside seawall with full soft armouring would cost, by an admittedly rough estimate, between sixty and ninety million dollars.

"The seawall is not coming down in our lifetime. But the next pier, the next stretch we replace, that one can be soft. We can stop adding to the line and start subtracting from it." Coastal engineer, Vancouver, 2024

This is what realism on the inlet looks like. Not the removal of Stanley Park's seawall, which is, as a piece of public infrastructure and beloved promenade, almost certainly there for the next two hundred years. But the gradual not-rebuilding of it, in selected reaches, when capital repair cycles come up. Replacing a section of failing concrete with a graded beach is more expensive in the short term and cheaper across the life of the asset, because the beach is more or less self-maintaining and the wall is not. That is the actuarial case for soft edges. The ecological case is the larger one. Both, oddly, point the same direction.

Sources District of West Vancouver · Ambleside Pier Sub-Tidal Habitat Project · Port of Vancouver, Maplewood Marine Restoration · Dethier et al., Estuaries and Coasts, 2016, "Multiscale impacts of shoreline armoring on Puget Sound."
